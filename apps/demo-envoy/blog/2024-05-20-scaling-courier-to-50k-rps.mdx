In January, Courier handled 800 relays per second. By March, a new customer onboarded with a webhook volume we had never planned for, and we needed to hit 50,000. That is not a tuning problem. That is a redesign.

This post covers the three architectural changes that got us there and the one thing we tried that made it worse.

## Where the Time Went

Before optimizing, we profiled. Every relay in Courier went through five stages: receive, authenticate, transform, route, deliver. We instrumented each stage with nanosecond-precision timing and ran 10,000 relays through the pipeline.

```text title="Relay pipeline — per-stage timing (p50 / p99)"
Stage          p50       p99
─────────────────────────────
Receive        0.3ms     0.8ms
Authenticate   1.2ms     4.1ms
Transform      0.4ms     1.3ms
Route          0.1ms     0.2ms
Deliver       12.7ms    89.3ms
─────────────────────────────
Total         14.7ms    95.7ms
```

Delivery dominated. Everything else was noise. The p99 was brutal — 89ms just for the outbound HTTP call, driven by slow destinations and TLS handshake variance. No amount of optimization to the receive or transform stages would move the needle.

## Change 1: Connection Pooling

Courier was opening a new TLS connection for every delivery. At 800 RPS, that meant 800 TLS handshakes per second. Each handshake took 15-40ms depending on the destination. We were spending more time negotiating encryption than delivering payloads.

The fix was a per-destination connection pool with keep-alive. Connections are established once, reused across deliveries, and recycled every 60 seconds to avoid stale connections. The pool size scales dynamically based on traffic volume to each destination.

| Metric           | Before Pooling | After Pooling |
|------------------|----------------|---------------|
| Delivery p50     | 12.7ms         | 1.4ms         |
| Delivery p99     | 89.3ms         | 8.2ms         |
| TLS handshakes/s | 800            | ~13           |
| Max throughput   | 2,100 RPS      | 18,000 RPS    |

That got us from 800 to 18,000. Not enough.

## Change 2: Dispatch Batching

Dispatch, the internal router, was processing relays one at a time. Each relay entered the pipeline, flowed through all five stages, and exited before the next one started. At 18,000 RPS, the pipeline was spending more time context-switching between relays than processing them.

We introduced micro-batching: Dispatch collects incoming relays for up to 500 microseconds, then flushes the batch through the pipeline as a group. Relays in the same batch that share a destination get their deliveries coalesced — one connection, multiple payloads, one round trip.

This got us to 38,000 RPS.

## Change 3: Sharded Ledger Writes

Ledger records every relay — receive time, authentication result, transformation output, delivery status, receipt. At 38,000 RPS, the single-writer Ledger became the bottleneck. Write latency spiked to 200ms at the p99, which back-pressured the entire pipeline.

We sharded Ledger writes by destination hash. Eight write shards, each handling a subset of destinations, each with its own write-ahead log. Writes became parallel. The p99 dropped to 3ms.

```text title="Throughput progression"
Baseline:                    800 RPS
+ Connection pooling:     18,000 RPS
+ Dispatch batching:      38,000 RPS
+ Sharded Ledger writes:  53,000 RPS
```

## The Thing That Made It Worse

We tried async delivery — fire the outbound request and immediately return success to the caller, recording the delivery result later. On paper, this should have doubled throughput by decoupling the delivery stage from the pipeline.

In practice, it introduced a correctness problem. When delivery is async, the relay response to the caller cannot include the delivery status. The caller thinks the message was relayed, but it might still be in flight. If it fails, the caller has no way to know without polling Ledger.

We ran it for three days. Two customers filed bugs because their receipt confirmation callbacks were arriving before Courier had finished delivering. We reverted and decided that synchronous delivery with connection pooling was the right tradeoff. Throughput is important. Correctness is mandatory.

## Where We Are Now

Courier handles 53,000 relays per second on a single Vial instance. For most deployments, that is more than enough. For the customer that triggered this work, we run three instances behind a Trellis load balancer, giving them 150,000+ RPS with automatic failover.

The total engineering time was six weeks. The total lines of code changed was 340. Most of the improvement came from not doing unnecessary work — not opening connections we did not need to open, not processing relays we could batch, not writing to a log we could shard.

Performance work is almost always about removal.
