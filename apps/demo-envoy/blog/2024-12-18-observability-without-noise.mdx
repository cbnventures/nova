We had 14,000 metrics. Twelve dashboards. Four alert channels. And when the Spoke endpoint went down at 11:23 PM on a Thursday, nobody noticed for forty minutes because the signal was buried under noise.

The problem was not a lack of observability. It was too much of it.

## The Noise Problem

Every relay in Courier emits metrics: receive time, auth time, transform time, route time, deliver time, payload size, response code, retry count, destination, source, event type. Multiply that by 30,000 relays per second and you get a firehose of data points that is technically comprehensive and practically useless.

Our Glassboard dashboards looked impressive. They had graphs for everything. But "everything" meant that when delivery latency to one destination spiked, it was one line among forty on a chart that nobody had looked at in three days.

```text title="Alert volume — Q3 2024"
Month     Alerts Fired    Actionable    False Positives    Missed Incidents
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
July           312             41              267                4
August         287             38              244                3
September      341             52              282                7
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Total          940            131              793               14
```

131 actionable alerts out of 940 total. That is a 14% signal rate. The other 86% trained the on-call engineer to ignore notifications.

## The Three Rules

We rewrote our alerting from scratch with three rules:

1. **Alert on symptoms, not causes.** Do not alert when CPU is high. Alert when delivery latency breaches SLA. CPU is a cause. Latency is a symptom. Customers experience symptoms.
2. **One alert, one action.** Every alert must have a clear, documented response. If the response is "look at the dashboard and figure it out," the alert is not ready.
3. **Silence is a signal.** If an alert has not fired in 90 days, delete it. Alerts that never fire are either misconfigured or monitoring something that does not break. Either way, they are noise.

## The New Stack

Glassboard now tracks five aggregate metrics per destination:

- **Delivery rate** (messages per second)
- **Error rate** (non-2xx responses per second)
- **Latency** (p50 and p99 delivery time)
- **Queue depth** (messages waiting for delivery)
- **Dead-letter count** (messages that exhausted retries in the last hour)

That is it. Five numbers per destination. If any of them cross their threshold, Glassboard fires a single alert through Courier with enough context to diagnose the issue without opening a dashboard.

```text title="Glassboard alert payload — delivered via Courier"
{
  "severity": "critical",
  "destination": "spoke://crm-sync",
  "trigger": "error_rate",
  "value": 42.3,
  "threshold": 5.0,
  "window": "60s",
  "context": {
    "delivery_rate": 180.7,
    "latency_p99": "340ms",
    "queue_depth": 1247,
    "dead_letters_1h": 89,
    "top_error": "503 Service Unavailable (78%)",
    "sample_message_id": "msg_f8a2c1d4"
  }
}
```

The on-call engineer gets one notification. It tells them what is broken, how broken it is, and where to start looking. No dashboard required for triage.

## Results

After the rewrite, we ran both systems in parallel for 30 days:

| Metric                   | Old Alerting | New Alerting |
|--------------------------|--------------|--------------|
| Alerts per month         | 312          | 23           |
| Actionable rate          | 14%          | 91%          |
| Missed incidents         | 4            | 0            |
| Mean time to detect      | 11 min       | 47 sec       |
| On-call fatigue (survey) | 7.2/10       | 2.1/10       |

Twenty-three alerts in a month. Twenty-one of them were actionable. Zero missed incidents. Mean time to detect dropped from eleven minutes to forty-seven seconds.

## The Lesson

Observability is not about collecting data. It is about surfacing the right data at the right time. Every metric you add to a dashboard, every alert you configure, every notification channel you enable — each one costs attention. And attention is the scarcest resource on the on-call rotation.

Five metrics. One alert. Enough context to act. That is the entire observability philosophy.
