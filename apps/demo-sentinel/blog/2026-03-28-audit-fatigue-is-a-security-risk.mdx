We talk to security teams that spend 40% of their time preparing for audits. Not responding to incidents. Not improving their security posture. Preparing evidence binders for auditors who will review them once and file them. The time spent on compliance preparation is time not spent on security — and that tradeoff is itself a security risk.

Audit fatigue is not a morale problem. It is an attack surface.

## The Cost of Manual Evidence Collection

A typical SOC 2 Type II audit requires evidence across 60 to 80 control points. For each control, the security team must produce documentation showing that the control exists, that it is enforced, and that it has been operating correctly for the entire audit period. In most organizations, this evidence is collected manually:

- Screenshots of access control configurations
- Exports from identity provider dashboards
- Spreadsheets mapping users to roles to resources
- Log excerpts showing that monitoring is active
- Change management records from ticketing systems
- Signed attestations from system owners

Each piece of evidence is a point-in-time snapshot. The screenshot shows the access control configuration as it existed on the day the screenshot was taken. It does not show whether the configuration was the same yesterday, or last month, or at any point during the audit period.

The manual process takes our customers an average of 320 engineering hours per audit cycle. That is eight weeks of a senior engineer's time — time that produces zero security value.

## The Real Risk

The security risk is not that audits are tedious. The risk is that the time spent on audit preparation displaces the time available for actual security work. We tracked how one customer's security team spent their time before and after deploying Spyglass:

```text title="Security team time allocation"
Before Spyglass:
  Audit preparation:     40%
  Incident response:     15%
  Policy review:         10%
  Threat modeling:        5%
  Tooling improvement:   10%
  Meetings/coordination: 20%

After Spyglass:
  Audit preparation:      5%
  Incident response:     20%
  Policy review:         20%
  Threat modeling:       15%
  Tooling improvement:   20%
  Meetings/coordination: 20%
```

The team did not get bigger. The same people are now spending 35% of their time on policy review, threat modeling, and tooling improvement instead of collecting screenshots. That is 35% of a security team's capacity redirected from compliance theater to actual defense.

## The Principle

Compliance evidence should be a byproduct of security operations, not a separate workstream. If you are doing security correctly — logging decisions, evaluating policies continuously, rotating keys on schedule, simulating changes before deployment — the audit evidence already exists. You just need a system that records it continuously and exports it on demand.

That is what Spyglass does. Not because audits are important (they are), but because the alternative — a security team that spends 40% of its time on evidence collection — is a security team that is 40% less effective at its actual job.
