Every marketing team starts with good intentions. The first campaign gets a clean name. The second one follows the same pattern. By the fifteenth campaign, someone is in a hurry and names it `test-new-thing-v3`. By the fiftieth, the naming convention exists only in the memory of the person who left six months ago.

We built Flare's campaign taxonomy system after watching this pattern repeat across hundreds of accounts. The goal was not to impose a rigid naming scheme — it was to make the right name easier to create than the wrong one.

## The Taxonomy

Flare enforces a four-segment campaign identifier:

```
{product}-{objective}-{audience}-{sequence}
```

Each segment has a constrained vocabulary defined by the team:

- **Product**: The product or feature being promoted (`core`, `enterprise`, `addon-sso`).
- **Objective**: The goal of the campaign (`awareness`, `activation`, `retention`, `expansion`).
- **Audience**: The target segment (`prospect`, `trial`, `customer`, `churned`).
- **Sequence**: A monotonically increasing number per product-objective-audience combination.

A real campaign name looks like `core-activation-trial-003`. It is not beautiful. It does not need to be. It needs to be unambiguous, sortable, and mergeable.

## Why Four Segments

Three segments is not enough — you cannot distinguish between an awareness campaign for prospects and an awareness campaign for churned users. Five segments adds a dimension that teams cannot consistently fill. Four is the minimum that captures product, intent, audience, and sequence without requiring a lookup table.

We tested three-segment, four-segment, and five-segment taxonomies across 40 accounts over six months:

| Segments | Adoption Rate | Naming Collisions | Abandoned After 90 Days |
|----------|---------------|-------------------|-------------------------|
| 3        | 78%           | 14%               | 31%                     |
| 4        | 82%           | 3%                | 12%                     |
| 5        | 61%           | 1%                | 44%                     |

Four segments hit the sweet spot: high adoption, low collisions, and low abandonment. Five segments had the fewest collisions but the highest abandonment rate — teams gave up on filling the fifth field consistently.

## Flare Enforces the Convention

When a team member creates a new campaign in Flare, the interface presents dropdown menus for each segment rather than a free-text field. New vocabulary terms require approval from a campaign admin. This eliminates the two most common failure modes:

1. **Typos and variations.** You cannot misspell `activation` if you are selecting it from a list.
2. **Unauthorized terms.** A new product name does not appear in the dropdown until someone adds it to the vocabulary.

The sequence number is assigned automatically. Teams never need to check what number comes next.

## Connecting Names to Attribution

Campaign names flow directly into Beacon's attribution metadata. When Prism runs an attribution query, it can aggregate by any segment of the taxonomy:

- "Show me all `activation` campaigns regardless of product or audience."
- "Show me all campaigns targeting `churned` users across every objective."
- "Show me the performance trend for `core` campaigns over the last four quarters."

These queries are only possible when campaign names follow a consistent structure. With free-text naming, the same queries require manual mapping tables that are always out of date.

## Start Early

The best time to adopt a naming convention is before your first campaign. The second-best time is now. Flare can retroactively map existing campaign names to the taxonomy using alias rules, but the mapping is manual and error-prone for accounts with hundreds of legacy names.

A naming convention is not overhead. It is infrastructure. Build it once and every downstream report, every attribution model, and every budget review becomes possible without a data cleaning sprint.
