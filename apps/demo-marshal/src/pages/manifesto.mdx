# Manifesto

Marshal exists because records are how a system tells the truth about itself. We believe a system without verifiable history is a system without accountability — and that the difference between a record and a guess is whether it can be pulled up six months later in a dispute.

## Records Over Summaries

A summary is an interpretation; an interpretation is a guess; a guess does not survive scrutiny six months later. Records do. We write down what happened, plainly, dated, and we resist the temptation to tell the reader what it meant. That is downstream work.

## Plain, Dated, Surviving

Records should be readable by someone who does not yet know what they are reading for. They should carry their own date. They should outlast the person who wrote them, the project that produced them, and the tooling that captured them.

## Verifiability as Practice

Verifiability is not a feature of certain records — it is a discipline applied to all of them. Every record is one piece of evidence in a future dispute. The system that cannot prove what happened is the system that does not know what it did.

### What the record owes

The record owes the reader an unambiguous account of an event. It does not owe a defense. It does not owe a narrative. It owes accuracy.
